Australia Burning How Climate Change Reshaped Australia's Bushfire Crisis

Australia has always lived with fire — but the fires burning across this continent today are something different. Drawing on NASA satellite fire records and over a century of Bureau of Meteorology climate data, this visualisation explores how rising temperatures, prolonged drought, and longer fire seasons are transforming Australia's ancient relationship with fire into a worsening crisis. The 2019–20 Black Summer brought that transformation into sharp focus: nearly nineteen million hectares burned, billions of animals displaced, and smoke that turned the skies of Sydney and Melbourne orange for weeks. This is the story of how we got here, and where the data says we are heading.

Section 01 — Australia Has Always Burned

Fire Is Part of Australia —
But Not Like This

Fire has shaped the Australian continent for millions of years. Indigenous Australians practiced cultural burning for over 65,000 years. But NASA's satellite fire record, which began in 2005, reveals something new: a dramatic and sustained escalation in fire intensity concentrated in the country's most populated southeast.

Dataset A NASA FIRMS — fire brightness, FRP, location, date (2005–2020)
Dataset B BOM / CSIRO — temperature anomaly, rainfall, FFDI (1910–2023)
Dataset C ABS / WWF / AIDR — human and wildlife impacts

The 2019 fire season was unlike anything Australia's satellite record had previously captured. From September through December, NASA's MODIS sensors aboard the Terra and Aqua satellites detected over 148,000 individual fire hotspots across the continent — each one a location where the land surface was radiating heat intense enough to trigger the sensor's thermal anomaly threshold. That is not a count of fires. It is a count of moments, each lasting minutes, each representing a pocket of active combustion somewhere on the Australian landscape.

What distinguishes 2019 from every prior year in this dataset is not just quantity but geography. Previous high-activity years — particularly in the Northern Territory — were driven by annual savanna burning, a predictable and ecologically managed cycle. The fires of 2019 were different: they burned deep into the temperate forests of the Great Dividing Range, through ecosystems that had not experienced significant fire in decades, and at intensities — measured by Fire Radiative Power — that shattered prior benchmarks. The map below encodes that heat: larger, darker circles mean more total energy released. New South Wales sits as a stark outlier.

Section 02 — A Warming Climate

Australia's Temperature Has
Risen +1.53°C Since 1910

Australia's climate has warmed significantly and unevenly over the past century. The warming accelerated after 1980. Every single year from 2013 to 2023 recorded above-average temperatures. The three hottest years on record — 2019, 2023, and 2013 — also coincide with Australia's worst fire seasons. This is not coincidence.

Section 03 — Drought Creates Fuel

Reduced Rainfall Dries
Forests Into Tinder

Reduced rainfall dries out forests and grasslands, turning vast areas into combustible fuel. Australia's rainfall is naturally variable, but a sustained drying trend since the 1970s — particularly across the southeast — has left soil moisture at historically low levels and vegetation stressed before fire season even begins.

Visualisation 3 — Monthly Rainfall Anomaly by Decade, Southern Australia 1960s–2020s (Matrix Heatmap)
Custom matrix heatmap: rows = calendar months, columns = decades, colour = average rainfall anomaly relative to the 1961–1990 baseline. Blue = above-average; orange/red = deficit. This matrix reveals the general drying trend and the asymmetric seasonal drying (summer and autumn showing the steepest deficits) simultaneously — something no standard bar or line chart can do.
Dataset B Source: BOM — Australian Rainfall and Runoff, monthly climatology 1960–2023 — bom.gov.au/water/awid · CSIRO — State of the Climate 2022 — csiro.au/State-of-climate
Section 04 — Fire Seasons Are Expanding

Fire Season Now Spans
Almost the Entire Year

Bushfire seasons are no longer confined to summer. In many regions, dangerous fire conditions now persist from late winter through to late autumn. This "season creep" stretches volunteer fire agencies across ever-longer deployments, shrinks windows for prescribed burning, and prevents ecosystems from recovering between events.

"The window of dangerous fire weather has expanded by 3–4 weeks across southern Australia since 1950."— Climate Council of Australia, 2023

The polar chart to the right arranges monthly fire danger data around a cyclic clock face, making seasonal patterns immediately intuitive. The solid green ring represents the official **low-to-moderate safety barrier (FFDI 12)**. Historically, fire danger stayed safely within this boundary for most of the year. However, comparing decades reveals a troubling trajectory: the bars are aggressively pushing well over this safety rating. While summer months have always breached it, the danger window is now expanding deep into autumn, forcing March, April, and May far past the safety threshold.

The circular structure of the chart is highly functional rather than decorative—it is the most appropriate visual encoding for cyclic, recurring seasonal data. It allows you to see the exact moment the fire season breaks through the safety barrier at a single glance, in a way a linear bar chart cannot easily replicate.

Reading this chart: Each wedge represents one month of the year. The length of the bar represents the 90th percentile Forest Fire Danger Index (FFDI), while color intensity reinforces the severity. The **solid green ring** marks the safety barrier limit. By using the dropdown to compare older decades against the 2010s, you can visually track how many months have transitioned from being safely contained to soaring well over the safety rating. Hovering over a month will explicitly show if its climate status is within or over the safety boundary.

Section 05 — The Black Summer Catastrophe

2019–20: A Season That
Changed Everything

The 2019–2020 Black Summer fires became the most destructive environmental disaster in Australia's recorded history. What made it different was not just its size, but the fact that it burned simultaneously across five states, in regions that had not burned in living memory, at intensities that exceeded every previous measurement.

Section 06 — Climate and Fire Intensity

Hotter Years Mean
More Intense Fires

The relationship between temperature and fire activity is not just intuitive — it is statistically measurable. Plotting annual temperature anomaly against total satellite-detected fire events shows a clear positive correlation: as temperatures rise above baseline, fire activity increases. 2019 sits as a clear outlier at both extremes.

Section 07 — Smoke Across Cities

Fire Smoke Spread Across
the Entire Continent

Smoke pollution from major fires spread far beyond the burn zones, affecting millions of Australians in cities hundreds of kilometres away. Sydney, Melbourne, and Canberra experienced air quality classified as "hazardous" for weeks during Black Summer. The health consequences — respiratory illness, premature death — extended the disaster far beyond what satellite fire maps show.

Visualisation 8 — Relative Fire Activity by State, 2005–2020: Streamgraph
Streamgraph idiom: area marks with stack="center" (the centre-symmetric stacking that defines a streamgraph). Each coloured band represents one state's contribution to total fire activity. The organic, wave-like visual shows how fire activity flows across different states over time. The 2019–20 surge is visible as a dramatic expansion of the NSW (red) band.
Dataset A Source: NASA FIRMS — annual fire radiative power by state (relative units), 2005–2020 — kaggle.com/datasets/nagarajbhat

Section 09 — Future Fire Risk

Under High Emissions, Extreme
Fire Days Could Double by 2050

CSIRO climate modelling offers two trajectories. Under the high-emissions scenario (RCP 8.5), extreme fire weather days nearly double by 2050 and continue rising. Under rapid decarbonisation (RCP 2.6), the increase is limited to approximately 30%. The space between these two paths is the direct, quantified consequence of climate decisions made in the next decade.

Visualisation 11 — Projected Increase in Extreme Fire Days by 2050 (RCP 8.5): State Choropleth Map
Choropleth map — a deliberately different idiom from Visualisations 1 and 6 (which use proportional symbols). The choropleth fills entire state polygons with colour encoding the projected % increase in extreme fire days by 2050. Choropleth is the correct idiom here because projected climate risk is a regional attribute (belonging to the whole state), not a point-located observation. ACT (88%) and VIC (82%) face the steepest increases.
Dataset B Source: CSIRO — Climate Change in Australia regional projections, RCP 8.5 — climatechangeinaustralia.gov.au · BOM — Regional Climate Futures — bom.gov.au/climate/change

Australia has always been a continent of fire. Indigenous Australians managed country with fire for over 65,000 years — a sophisticated, life-sustaining practice built on ecological knowledge no satellite has yet replicated. But the fire we measure today from space is different in scale, in heat intensity, and in the months it chooses. Two datasets, eleven visualisations, one conclusion: what is happening is not cyclical. It is a trend — and the data shows exactly where it leads.
